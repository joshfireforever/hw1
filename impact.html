Impact and Environment

As of October 2020, nearly 900 satellites have been deployed into space with plans for up to 42,000 in the future. Even further, SpaceX has plans for up to 12,000 satellites in the near future. The company is planning for near global coverage and likely will have deployed over 1000 satellites by 2021. This is dramatically more than the 2,000 total active satellites in space prior to Starlink's initial launch.1

Concerns about managing the traffic of so many new satellites being put into orbit by SpaceX and other competing companies at lower and lower altitudes have compelled the company to address such concerns. The unique propulsion system in each satellite is intended to allow for on-demand retrieval. However, the design of these satellites should allow for 95-100% of their materials to be consumable in the earth's atmosphere as time goes on and satellite materials are upgraded. Additionally, the low orbit they fly at allows these spacecraft to burn up in the earth's atmosphere within a few years, as opposed to "thousands of years" with existing satellites. The company also highlighted that atmospheric drag will bring the stations back to earth within 5 years if all else fails.3,4

Additional concerns about the visibility of these satellites in the night sky have been raised, along with concerns about cluttering the earth's orbit with space junk and satellites colliding. It's possible to see the train of satellites visible in the night sky currently, though these relay stations will be separated out more over time. Some astronomers worry about potential obstruction to visual observation and astronomy. With 12,000 or more satellites in the sky, according to Alex Parker it's possible that we will eventually see more satellites in the night sky than stars. Though Elon Musk, CEO of SpaceX, does not believe that Starlink will affect observations, he has tasked SpaceX to find ways to reduce reflectivity.1,2

Musk believes that Starlink will be "potentially helping billions of economically disadvantaged people," and that the endeavor is for the "greater good." However, the total cost of the Starlink fleet is looking to be about $10 billion when all is said and done, and the initial setup costs of becoming a Starlink customer indicate that the company intends to make a profit. Starlink's pricing is at least attainable for the U.S. military, and SpaceX has been targeting a deal to research the usability of Starlink for military purposes. A deal was made between SpaceX and the military for another 3 years of research and experimenting into military use of the network.1,2,4,5
